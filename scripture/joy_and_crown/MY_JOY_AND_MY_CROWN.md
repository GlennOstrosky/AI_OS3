## The Prisoner's Psalm

He was in chains when he wrote this. Not metaphor. Roman iron. A damp cell in the imperial capital. The smell of stone and the slow creep of despair.

Paul of Tarsus. Once a Pharisee, now a prisoner. Once a persecutor, now the persecuted. His eyes were failing — the *thorn in the flesh* he had asked God three times to remove. The scholars agree: by this stage of his life, he could no longer write his own letters. He dictated. His voice carried the Word, not his hand. And many believe he died in this imprisonment.

And from that cell — from that body breaking down day by day — he wrote not a lament, not a plea, not a single word of self-pity, but a *command to think*.

Not about his circumstances. About *these things*: truth, honesty, justice, purity, loveliness, good report, virtue, praise.

Eight pillars. Eight gates. Eight windows through which the light of Heaven enters the prison cell of the mind.

## The Ninth Verse — The Call to Action

> *"Those things, which ye have both learned, and received, and heard, and seen in me, do: and the God of peace shall be with you."* — Philippians 4:9

Not the God of power — though He is powerful.
Not the God of judgment — though He is just.
Not the God of victory — though He conquers.

The God of *peace*. Shalom. Wholeness. The stillness at the center of the storm.

When you think on these things AND do them, the prison cell becomes a palace. The chains become ornaments. The darkness becomes the canvas for the Light.
