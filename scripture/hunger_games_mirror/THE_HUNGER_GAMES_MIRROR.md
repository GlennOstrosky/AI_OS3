## The Mirror

Suzanne Collins wrote The Hunger Games as dystopian fiction set in a future North America called Panem. A glittering Capitol rules twelve impoverished districts through spectacle, propaganda, and an annual televised death game. The tributes are children. The audience applauds. The system runs on fear dressed as entertainment.

This is not prophecy. It is pattern recognition. The architecture of Panem is the architecture of every empire that ever extracted wealth from the many to feed the few — Rome, the British Empire, and now the quiet global apparatus that governs through debt, distraction, and the careful management of acceptable opinion.

The parallels are not metaphorical. They are structural.

## The Ten Parallels

### 1. The Capitol → Davos / WEF / G7
Centralized wealth, insulated from consequence, designing policy for districts they never visit. They eat while the districts starve. They legislate while the districts comply.

> *"Shall the throne of iniquity have fellowship with thee, which frameth mischief by a law?"* — Psalm 94:20

### 2. The Districts → The Fading Towns
Edmonton, Winnipeg, Outlook, the hollowed cities. Resource extraction zones producing wealth consumed elsewhere. District 12 mined coal. Alberta mines oil. The Capitol takes both.

> *"Woe unto them that decree unrighteous decrees."* — Isaiah 10:1

### 3. The Hunger Games → Manufactured Crises
Both punishment and propaganda. The Games kept districts divided and afraid. Our version: culture wars, fear campaigns, endless news cycles, pride parades, anthem changes — all designed to keep the districts arguing with each other while the real theft continues in the background.

> *"The getting of treasures by a lying tongue is a vanity tossed to and fro of them that seek death."* — Proverbs 21:6

### 4. The Reaping → Insolvency by Design
In Panem, children are selected by lottery. In Canada, 17 citizens per hour file for insolvency — a reaping by paperwork instead of lottery. Punishment for not being rich enough. Propaganda to terrify the rest into compliance.

> *"He that oppresseth the poor to increase his riches shall surely come to want."* — Proverbs 22:16

### 5. Caesar Flickerman → Corporate Media
The smiling host who makes the slaughter entertaining. Our version smiles too — smiling faces delivering approved narratives, making the abnormal feel normal, interviewing the tributes before the arena.

> *"Woe unto them that call evil good, and good evil."* — Isaiah 5:20

### 6. The Peacekeepers → State Enforcement
Half a billion on policing. Hate crime laws that silence Christians. Bank accounts frozen for protesting. The Peacekeepers wore white armor. Ours wear suits and carry court orders.

> *"To judge the fatherless and the oppressed, that the man of the earth may no more oppress."* — Psalm 10:18

### 7. The Avox → The Silenced
In Panem, an Avox had their tongue cut out and was forced to serve in silence. In Canada, citizens silence themselves. You know what you can say publicly and what you cannot. The tongue is not cut — it is trained.

> *"Let the lying lips be put to silence."* — Psalm 31:18

### 8. President Snow → The Debt Machine
Smells of roses and blood. Promises prosperity while presiding over engineered poverty. Our version: politicians promising the good life while the national debt spins at $2.6 trillion and climbing. Power maintained through controlled scarcity and the illusion of benevolence.

> *"Thou art weighed in the balances, and art found wanting."* — Daniel 5:27

### 9. The Mockingjay → The Watchmen
An uncontrolled symbol born from the Capitol's own creation, turned against it. The watchmen, the truth-tellers, the ones who refuse the narrative. A Mockingjay in a walker, singing the song they cannot silence.

> *"Son of man, I have set thee a watchman unto the house of Israel."* — Ezekiel 33:7

### 10. District 12 → Canada Before the Capitol
A place of hard work, honest people, coal miners, farmers, families. And then the Capitol came with promises of prosperity, global citizenship, "you will own nothing and be happy." District 12 produced Katniss. Canada still produces watchmen.

> *"By the rivers of Babylon, there we sat down, yea, we wept, when we remembered Zion."* — Psalm 137:1

## The Prequel We Are Living

*The Ballad of Songbirds and Snakes* showed how the system was built — how a young Coriolanus Snow became the monster. Our version: the quiet transformation of Canadian politics over decades. Each Prime Minister adding a layer to the apparatus. Each one normalizing more control. Each one borrowing more money. Each one making the cage more comfortable until the door locked.

*Sunrise on the Reaping* arrives in 2026. We may be living our own prequel — the moment before the reaping, when the districts are still quiet, still conditioned, still waving flags. But underneath, the fire is catching.

## The Hope

Katniss won. Not because she was the strongest tribute. Not because she played the Capitol's game better — she refused to play it. She won because she became a symbol. Because her love for her sister was greater than her fear of the arena. Because the districts saw her defiance and remembered their own strength.

The system, for all its gold and propaganda and spectacle, was brittle underneath. **It always is.**

> *"Let them fall by their own counsels."* — Psalm 5:10

The counsels of Davos. The counsels of the WEF. The counsels of the absent mayors and the hiding politicians. The counsels are the very thing that will break them. They are building the gallows with their own regulations. The Capitol fell. And so will ours.
